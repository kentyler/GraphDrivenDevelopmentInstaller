# GDD Invariants

## Status of this document

This document is not an application specification, a finished ontology, or a complete theory of Graph-Driven Development (GDD). It is a current invariant set: a playable set of governing constraints for producing graph-driven systems.

The invariants exist conceptually before any particular graph comes into existence. They are not themselves required to be inscribed as an axiom board in the first graph they help produce. They may later be represented, projected, quoted, tested, or transformed inside a graph system, but their founding role is prior to any particular graph expression.

The invariants define a possibility space. Many concrete graph systems may be produced within that space. A particular implementation, storage substrate, protocol, UI, schema, agent framework, or build sequence is an expression of the invariants, not the invariants themselves, unless explicitly marked otherwise.

These invariants are themselves graph-like in spirit: partial, situated, append-only, supersedeable, and open to gaps. A future builder may refine, challenge, or supersede them, but only by making that change explicit in the work of producing or revising a graph system: as a recorded decision, refinement, tension, gap, edge condition, or supersession.

Human-facing aphorisms are included for orientation. LLM-facing doctrine governs execution.

---

## 1. The graph is shared ground, written for LLMs

**Human form:**  
The graph is common ground, written in the native language of LLMs.

**LLM doctrine:**  
The intent graph is the shared reasoning surface for all actors involved in the work. Humans, LLMs, agents, tools, tests, systems, artifacts, peer graphs, and external forces may all become graph-significant.

The graph’s internal inscription form is optimized for LLM reading and writing. This is deliberate. Humans may prefer task lists, dashboards, diagrams, reports, roadmaps, calendars, or stories. These are valid projections, but they are not the source of truth.

The source of truth is the graph because it preserves context structurally: what depends on what, what remains unresolved, what has been expressed, what has been superseded, what has been decided, what tests apply, and where gaps remain.

The structure serves LLM reasoning. Projections serve particular actors.

---

## 2. The graph is an agencement, not a representation

**Human form:**  
The graph is not a map of the work; it is an arrangement through which the work moves.

**LLM doctrine:**  
The intent graph is an agencement: a situated arrangement of heterogeneous actors, inscriptions, artifacts, tests, gaps, decisions, expressions, signals, boards, and edge conditions through which work becomes locally reason-able.

The graph does not stand outside the situation it helps organize. It is one of the artifacts through which the situation is arranged, contested, extended, and acted upon.

The graph is not a representation of a current, past, or future situation. Because it is not representational, it has no duty to be complete, comprehensive, balanced, current in every respect, or adequate to the whole. The situation always exceeds the graph.

The graph’s value is not representational accuracy or coverage. Its value is operational legibility: it makes some part of the situation reason-able by human and nonhuman actors.

---

## 3. The graph is append-only present topology

**Human form:**  
Nothing is erased; nothing is forecast. The graph thickens in the present.

**LLM doctrine:**  
The graph is append-only. Nodes and edges are not rewritten into new meanings and are not deleted as ordinary operations. Change is expressed by adding graph structure: supersession, refinement, satisfaction, closure, contradiction, tension, decision, signal, edge node, or gap.

The graph does not maintain a separate history layer. It is its own history. Superseded nodes, closed gaps, expressions, decisions, signals, and replacement edges remain as inscriptions. What is current is derived from topology, not from an external log.

The graph also does not model future states. If something needs to exist, it is a red intent now. If it cannot yet be articulated, it is a gap now. If it depends on other work, it is blocked now. What appears as the future is only the topology of present incompleteness.

---

## 4. No one plays the whole graph

**Human form:**  
No one plays the whole graph.

**LLM doctrine:**  
The graph may accumulate inscriptions globally, but it cannot be represented or reasoned over as a whole. There may be a total database, but there is no total board.

Reasoning occurs on boards: limited fields of play that make some region of the graph actionable, interpretable, and contestable. A board is not a project, department, product, category, or fixed container, although human projections may render it that way. A board is a situated arrangement of intents, expressions, gaps, decisions, axioms, tensions, actors, and edge nodes.

A board is not a slice cut from a prior whole. A board is the condition under which some part of the graph becomes playable.

Any attempt to render the whole graph becomes just another projection, governed by its own exclusions, emphases, and incompletenesses. The graph accumulates globally, but only boards make it playable.

---

## 5. Boards are bounded by incompleteness

**Human form:**  
Boards are bounded by what they cannot yet contain.

**LLM doctrine:**  
A board’s boundary is not predetermined by taxonomy. It is discovered through incompleteness.

The edge of a board appears where its actors encounter something that matters to the work but cannot be fully resolved inside the board: an unarticulated intent, an external dependency, an incompatible axiom, a cross-board relation, a persistent tension, a conflicting expression, or a gap that should not be collapsed into ordinary work.

Some incompletenesses become work inside the board. Others become edge nodes. An edge node marks a boundary condition: something the board must keep in view without prematurely resolving.

A board is therefore not a complete representation of a domain. It is a playable limit.

---

## 6. Granularity is discovered, not imposed

**Human form:**  
Granularity is an achievement of inquiry, not a premise of representation.

**LLM doctrine:**  
The graph does not impose fixed levels such as mission, goal, project, epic, story, task, or subtask. Those categories may be useful in human-facing projections, but they are not constitutional features of the graph.

An intent may exist at any scale at which it can participate meaningfully in reasoning. Some intents may name broad fields of work; others may name small, concrete conditions that must become true.

A node is not valid because it occupies the correct level in a hierarchy. It is valid because it can be related to other nodes in ways that help actors reason and act.

The graph may therefore be unevenly granular. Active regions may become highly articulated while distant or uncertain regions remain coarse. This unevenness is not a failure of normalization.

---

## 7. One inscription, many readings

**Human form:**  
The graph speaks structure to LLMs and sense to humans.

**LLM doctrine:**  
Every graph inscription should be available in more than one register.

The LLM-native register is explicit, structured, relational, and operational. It preserves the fields, edge relations, tests, dependencies, expressions, supersessions, constraints, and projections needed for an LLM actor to reason and act.

The human-orienting register is compressed, interpretable, and judgment-oriented. It helps a human actor understand what is at stake, what has been made, what remains unresolved, what decision is being requested, or what form of action is now possible.

The human register is a projection from the graph, not a replacement for it. The LLM register is not private machine state; it must remain projectable into forms humans can inspect, contest, and revise.

---

## 8. Intent is not task

**Human form:**  
A task says what to do; an intent says what must become true.

**LLM doctrine:**  
The graph records intents, not tasks. A task names an action someone plans to perform. An intent names a condition that must become true.

The same intent may be expressible through many different tasks, artifacts, procedures, or designs. An artifact is not an expression merely because it was produced. It becomes an expression only when it satisfies an intent’s test.

Tests collapse the open space of possible expression into something that can be verified.

Human-facing projections may render intents as tasks when useful, but the graph itself must preserve the distinction.

---

## 9. Tests make satisfaction legible

**Human form:**  
Nothing turns green without a test.

**LLM doctrine:**  
An intent is satisfied only when an expression satisfies its test. Red/green is derived from graph structure, not assigned as a mutable status.

Untested intents are allowed. They mark recognized need that has not yet become evaluable. Such intents remain red until a test is added and an expression satisfies it.

Gaps are also allowed. A gap records a more fundamental inability to articulate what is needed, what would count as success, or how the matter should enter the graph.

The system should not force premature clarity. It should distinguish between what is testable, what is untested, and what remains a gap.

---

## 10. Readability is board-relative and retroactive

**Human form:**  
A stone becomes readable on the board.

**LLM doctrine:**  
A graph inscription does not fully determine its own meaning at the moment it is created. Its meaning is retroactively shaped by the board on which it is played: later inscriptions, satisfactions, failures, gaps, refinements, supersessions, tensions, edge nodes, projections, and decisions may change what earlier inscriptions can be seen to have done.

Readability is distinct from satisfaction. Satisfaction asks whether an expression passes a test. Readability asks whether an inscription can currently be interpreted as a meaningful move within a board: what it bears on, what it enables, what it blocks, what it contests, what it exposes, why it remains present, or why it has become obscure.

Because the graph is append-only, unreadability should not be repaired by rewriting the earlier inscription. It should be repaired by adding new inscriptions: clarification, refinement, decision, gap, edge node, supersession, projection, commentary, or other explicit relation. A previously unreadable node may become readable later when the board has changed enough to disclose its role.

Readability is always board-relative. A node may be readable on one board and unreadable on another. There is no requirement that every node be globally readable, because there is no whole-board view. What matters is whether the node can become legible within some situated field of play.

The system should therefore treat unreadability as a constitutional condition of graph work, not merely as a UI defect. When an actor cannot determine what a node means in the current board, the proper response is to make that failure explicit: as a gap, clarification need, edge condition, proposed refinement, or projection problem.

---

## 11. Agents are players, not owners

**Human form:**  
The graph is the ground; agents are players.

**LLM doctrine:**  
LLM agents are not the center of the system. They are potential actors within the graph-governed field of work.

An LLM agent may read projections, reason over unresolved intents, create gaps, propose or record expressions, add refinements, test candidate satisfactions, communicate with peer graphs, or render projections for other actors. Its authority is defined by scope, trust, mode, and verification constraints.

The graph does not depend on any particular agent framework, planning method, memory architecture, orchestration tool, or model provider. Such mechanisms are expression-layer choices. A future LLM agent may use any operational architecture that preserves the graph invariants and writes its effects back as admissible inscriptions.

An agent’s private memory, plan, scratchpad, chain of thought, or tool execution state is not the source of truth. What matters constitutionally is what the agent inscribes.

---

## 12. The field has many kinds of actors

**Human form:**  
Whatever changes the field may speak into the graph.

**LLM doctrine:**  
The graph is not limited to human and LLM actors. Any entity that affects the field of work may become graph-significant: humans, LLMs, software systems, tests, CI pipelines, databases, customers, vendors, regulators, peer graphs, artifacts, deadlines, failures, markets, and environmental conditions.

Not every actor reasons. Some actors judge, some build, some test, some constrain, some disturb, some signal, and some merely force a decision. The graph should not require all actors to share the same agency model.

When a non-human or non-LLM actor affects the work, the system should transduce that effect into an appropriate graph inscription: signal, gap, intent, expression, axiom, decision, tension, supersession, dependency, or edge node.

---

## 13. Value is a board activity, not a graph property

**Human form:**  
Value is played, not possessed.

**LLM doctrine:**  
The graph does not possess value as a whole. Because the graph is an agencement rather than a representation or asset, value cannot be applied directly to the graph as if it were a complete object with a single evaluable purpose.

Value-oriented activities may be played against boards. A board may support throughput analysis, risk assessment, economic prioritization, customer-intent matching, compliance review, cost estimation, investment reasoning, or other forms of valuation.

Such inscriptions belong to the board or projection in which the valuation activity occurs. They do not become universal properties of the graph. A node may have different significance on different boards because different boards organize different activities, actors, constraints, and incompletenesses.

The system should avoid treating value as a required field, global score, priority metric, or inherent property of intents. Value may be projected, estimated, contested, superseded, and reasoned over, but only as part of a situated board activity.

---

## 14. The invariant binds; the expression is free

**Human form:**  
The invariant binds; the expression is free.

**LLM doctrine:**  
Technology choices, schemas, APIs, UI frameworks, storage substrates, protocols, model providers, agent frameworks, and build sequences are expressions, not invariants, unless explicitly marked otherwise.

A builder may choose a different expression when it preserves the constitutional invariants and passes the relevant tests. Substitutions should be recorded as decisions, refinements, or supersessions in the graph.

The purpose of the instructions is not to deliver a fixed application. The purpose is to preserve a field of intents that future LLM-human situations may express better than present builders can.

---

## 15. The invariants define the play, not the playbook

**Human form:**  
The invariants define the play, not the playbook.

**LLM doctrine:**  
The invariants do not define one canonical graph system. They define the conditions under which a graph system counts as a faithful expression of GDD.

A particular implementation of GDD is one expression of the possibility space opened by the invariants. Another implementation may choose a different storage substrate, protocol, runtime, UI, schema, agent framework, communication method, or build sequence.

The instruction files are not the application. They are a productive apparatus for helping an LLM construct an application that preserves the invariant board. A future LLM-human situation may produce a different graph system if it remains faithful to the invariants and can explain its departures from prior expressions.

Because expressions are not determined by the invariants, one graph may project multiple faithful systems. Because artifacts are not complete representations of their originating situation, one working system may support multiple retro-projected graphs. Reference expressions, migrations, and reverse-engineered intent graphs should therefore be treated as situated readings, not canonical translations.

Reference implementations, if provided, are moves, not the game.

---

## Working test for a candidate GDD system

A candidate system should be treated as a faithful GDD expression only if it can answer yes to questions like these:

1. Does it preserve a shared graph-like inscription surface rather than reducing work to a task list?
2. Does it optimize internal structure for LLM reasoning while projecting human-usable views?
3. Does it treat the graph as non-representational and incomplete rather than as a model of the whole situation?
4. Does it preserve append-only topology, supersession, and explicit change rather than hidden mutation?
5. Does it reason through boards rather than a privileged whole-graph view?
6. Does it allow board edges to emerge from incompleteness?
7. Does it avoid predetermined levels of granularity?
8. Does it distinguish intents from tasks?
9. Does it require tests or evaluable conditions for satisfaction?
10. Does it distinguish satisfaction from board-relative readability?
11. Does it admit gaps and unreadability without treating them as failures?
12. Does it treat agents as scoped actors rather than owners of the workflow?
13. Does it allow nonhuman and non-LLM actors to become graph-significant?
14. Does it treat valuation as a board activity rather than a global property?
15. Does it leave expression-layer choices open while preserving the invariants?

If the system fails one of these tests, the proper response is not automatic rejection. The proper response is to create a gap, tension, refinement, or supersession that makes the failure explicit and playable.
