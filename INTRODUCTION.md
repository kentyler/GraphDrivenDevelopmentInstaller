# Introduction

Intent graphs represent the field of work in a form LLMs can reason over. In order to engage an LLM as a full partner in work, we conceptualize the work not as a list of tasks but as a cluster of intentions. A task says what someone plans to do; an intent says what must become true. We begin with intents because the same intent may be expressible through many different tasks and artifacts. Each intent opens a space of possible actions and possible artifacts. A test constrains that space by specifying what would count as a satisfactory expression of the intent. Actions then produce artifacts -- code, documents, schemas, interfaces, procedures -- and those artifacts are tested to determine whether they actually express one or more intents. An artifact is not an expression merely because it was produced; it becomes an expression only when it satisfies the intent's test. These clusters of intentions form organized fields that we treat as "games" played on "boards." The intents on a board can be related to one another, but no single core intent can fully explain the board, because what binds the board together is not a unifying purpose but a structured field of tensions, dependencies, and oppositions. A board is not the container for a unified purpose; it is the surface on which incompatible but mutually implicated intents become playable. A set of intents can produce many possible sets of artifacts, and a single artifact can express multiple intents, including intents from different boards. Boards communicate with each other, either locally or over the network, by prompting with descriptions of their unresolved intents and responding with high-level descriptions of patterns of intent that might resolve the intents in the prompt. The system is offered not as an already constructed artifact, but as a set of skills any LLM can use to construct the infrastructure.

The intent graph is a Deleuzian map -- made from inside the process, adding to the territory rather than reproducing it. It is a moment of the whole's self-differentiation, not a representation of it. This is what separates it from both high-resolution modeling of a closed totality (where the system is assumed to be already there, waiting to be mapped at finer grain) and naive immersive processism that refuses structure altogether. The intent graph is write-only and accumulating. Intents are superseded, never deleted. Expressions attach to intents but never replace them. The graph does not converge on a final picture; it thickens. Each new node participates in the field it records rather than representing a field that exists independently of it.
